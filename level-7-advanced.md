# Level 7: Advanced

**Timeline:** Year 2-3+
**CCOM Grade:** Grade 7-9
**Goal:** Concert-level playing, artistic mastery, personal voice

---

## Music Theory for This Level

### Contemporary Chinese Composition Techniques
- Modern composers (Guo Wenjing, Tan Dun, Chen Yi) have expanded dizi vocabulary
- Extended techniques: multiphonics, key clicks, air sounds, singing while playing
- Non-traditional notation: graphic notation, aleatoric (chance) passages
- Fusion of Chinese and Western compositional methods

### Extended Techniques Notation
- Symbols for: multiphonics, air tones, key clicks, throat sounds
- Box notation for aleatoric passages
- Graphic notation for timbral effects
- Each contemporary composer may use their own notation system — always read the performance notes

### Masterwork Analysis
How to deeply study a piece:
1. Historical context: when was it written, why, by whom?
2. Musical structure: map out all sections
3. Modal analysis: what mode/key, where does it modulate?
4. Technical demands: what techniques are required?
5. Interpretive choices: compare 3+ recordings, note differences
6. Personal interpretation: what do YOU want to say with this piece?

### Aesthetics of Chinese Music
- **意境 (Yi Jing — Artistic Conception)**: The intangible "world" a piece creates. Beyond notes and technique, it's the spirit and atmosphere. A performer must enter the yi jing to convey it.
- **气韵 (Qi Yun — Spirit Resonance)**: The vitality and life-energy flowing through the music. Music without qi yun sounds technically correct but dead.
- **虚实 (Xu Shi — Empty and Full)**: The interplay of silence and sound, strong and gentle. Great music breathes.
- **留白 (Liu Bai — Leaving White)**: Like white space in Chinese painting — what you don't play is as important as what you do.

### Performance Interpretation
- Same piece, different performers = different experiences
- Interpretation is personal — there is no single "correct" way
- Study multiple masters' recordings of the same piece
- Develop your own interpretive voice through deep understanding of the music's cultural and emotional context

## Advanced Techniques

### Multiphonics
- Playing two notes simultaneously through special fingering + embouchure
- Creates ethereal, otherworldly sounds
- Used in contemporary compositions
- Requires very precise control
- Not part of traditional repertoire — a modern extended technique

### Throat Vibrato (喉振音)
- Vibrato produced by pulsing the throat/glottis
- Different quality from diaphragm vibrato
- Used for specific expressive effects
- Can be combined with breath vibrato
- Creates a more intense, focused vibrato than standard breath vibrato

### Extended Range
- Pushing beyond the standard range through harmonic fingerings
- Requires very refined embouchure control
- Not commonly needed but demonstrates mastery
- Some contemporary pieces demand notes above the standard third-octave range

### Memorization Strategies for Long Works
1. **Structural memory**: Know the form (散慢中快散) — you always know where you are
2. **Phrase memory**: Memorize phrase by phrase, link them into sections
3. **Finger memory**: Muscle memory through repetition (but never rely on this alone)
4. **Emotional memory**: Associate each section with an emotion or image
5. **Analytical memory**: Understand the harmonic/modal logic — predict what comes next

### Stage Performance Skills
- Confidence comes from thorough preparation
- Communication with audience through body language
- Managing performance anxiety: deep breathing, visualization, positive self-talk
- Program selection: balance pieces for contrast (fast/slow, northern/southern, old/new)

## Repertoire in Jianpu

### Song 1: 幽兰逢春 (Orchid Meets Spring)
Grade 8-9. By Zhao Songting and Cao Xing (1979). One of the great dizi concertos. Originally performed with orchestra, also performed solo. Depicts the orchid (symbol of integrity) blooming after a long winter — a metaphor for renewal after hardship.

**散板 Introduction**
```
幽兰逢春 — 散板 Introduction
1=D  散板

0 0 0 5̣ | 6̣ - - - 1 2 | 3 - - 2 1 6̣ | 5̣ - - - |
6̣ 1 2 3 | 5 - - 3 2 | 1 - - 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ - - - - |
```
The orchid in winter — fragile, lonely, but alive. Pianissimo, with a searching quality. Each phrase reaches upward and falls back, like a plant reaching for light.

**慢板 Slow Theme**
```
幽兰逢春 — 慢板 Slow Theme
1=D  4/4
Adagio ♩=46

6̣ - 1 2 | 3 - 2 1 | 6̣ - 5̣ 6̣ | 1 - - - |
2 - 3 5 | 6 - 5 3 | 2 - 1 6̣ | 5̣ - - - |
6̣ - 1 2 | 3 - 5 6 | 5 - 3 2 | 3 - - - |
2 - 1 6̣ | 5̣ - 6̣ 1 | 6̣ - - - | 0 - - - |
5̣ - 6̣ 1 | 2 - 3 2 | 1 - 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ - - - |
1 - 2 3 | 5 - 6 5 | 3 - 2 1 | 2 - - - |
6̣ - 1 2 | 3 - 2 1 | 6̣ - 5̣ - | 6̣ - - - ||
```
Full vibrato, extensive ornamentation, dynamic extremes. The slow section is heartbreakingly beautiful. This is the emotional core of the piece — the orchid's inner life, its patience and resilience through the long winter. Every note must sing with deep feeling. Southern-style ornamentation throughout — slides, grace notes, turns.

**快板 Fast Section**
```

1=D  2/4
Allegro ♩=144

3 5 6 5 | 3 2 1 6̣ | 5̣ 6̣ 1 2 | 3 - |
5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 1 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ - - - |
2 3 5 6 | 1̇ 6 5 3 | 5 6 1̇ 6 | 5 3 2 1 |
6̣ 1 2 3 | 5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 | 2 - |
3 2 1 6̣ | 5̣ 6̣ 1 2 | 3 5 6 5 | 3 2 1 6̣ |
5̣ - 6̣ - | 1 - - - ||
```
Spring arrives — energy, joy, rebirth. The contrast with the slow section is dramatic. Clean tonguing, rhythmic precision, building to an exhilarating climax. The orchid blooms at last.

### Song 2: 三五七 (San Wu Qi)
A Zhejiang opera (婺剧) style piece arranged by Zhao Songting. Named after its rhythmic pattern (groups of 3, 5, and 7). Grade 7.

**Opening (Rhythmic Theme)**
```
三五七 — Opening (Rhythmic Theme)
1=D  散板→2/4

0 5 3 | 2 1 6̣ 5̣ 3̣ | 5̣ - |
0 5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 3̣ 5̣ 6̣ | 1 - |
0 5 3 2 1 6̣ 5̣ | 3̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 2 3 5 | 6 - |
```
The groups of 3, 5, and 7 notes define the piece's identity. First phrase: 3 notes. Second: 5 notes. Third: 7 notes. This asymmetric structure comes from Zhejiang opera percussion patterns.

**Fast Section**
```
三五七 — Fast Section
1=D  2/4
Vivace ♩=152

5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 3̣ 5̣ | 6̣ 1 2 3 | 5 6 5 3 |
2 1 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ 1 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 3̣ 5̣ | 6̣ - |
5 6 5 3 | 2 3 2 1 | 6̣ 1 6̣ 5̣ | 3̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 |
2 3 5 3 | 2 1 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ 1 2 1 | 6̣ - |
3̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 | 2 3 5 6 | 5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 3̣ 5̣ |
6̣ - | 0 0 ||
```
The rhythmic groups of 3, 5, 7 (三五七) create the unique asymmetric drive of this piece. Fast, brilliant, theatrical. This is opera music — dramatic and extroverted.

### Song 3: 秦川抒怀 (Reflections on Qinchuan)
By Ma Di (马迪). A northern masterpiece evoking the landscapes of Shaanxi province. Grade 8. Uses Shang mode (商调式) — the Shaanxi regional character.

**散板**
```
秦川抒怀 — 散板
1=D  散板

0 0 2 3 | 5 - - 6 5 | 3 - 2 - | 1 - 6̣ - |
5̣ - - - | 6̣ 1 2 3 | 2 - - - - |
3 5 6 5 | 3 - 2 1 | 6̣ - 5̣ - | 6̣ - - - |
1 2 3 5 | 6 - 5 3 | 2 - - - - ||
```
The opening evokes the vast Qinchuan Plain — the ancient heartland of China. Shang mode gives it that unmistakable Shaanxi flavor — open, raw, emotional. The wide intervals reflect the wide-open landscape.

**Lyrical Theme**
```
秦川抒怀 — Lyrical Theme
1=D  4/4
Adagio ♩=50

2 - 3 5 | 6 - 5 3 | 2 - - 1 | 6̣ - - - |
5̣ - 6̣ 1 | 2 - 3 2 | 1 - 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ - - - |
2 - 3 5 | 6 - 1̇ 6 | 5 - 3 2 | 3 - - - |
5 - 6 1̇ | 6 - 5 3 | 2 - 1 6̣ | 5̣ - - - |
6̣ - 1 2 | 3 - 2 1 | 6̣ - 5̣ 6̣ | 1 - - - |
2 - 3 5 | 6 - 5 3 | 2 - - - | 0 - - - ||
```
The Shang mode (home note = 2) gives this piece its distinctive northwest Chinese character — open, desolate, and deeply emotional. Rich vibrato, slides between notes (especially sliding up to 2 and 5), and the characteristic Shaanxi ornamental patterns. This is the musical equivalent of standing on the ancient city walls of Xi'an, looking out over the plain.

**Fast Section**
```
秦川抒怀 — 快板
1=D  2/4
Vivace ♩=160

2 3 5 6 | 5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 1 2 3 | 2 1 6̣ 5̣ |
6̣ 1 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 | 2 - | 0 0 |
5 6 5 3 | 2 3 2 1 | 6̣ 1 6̣ 5̣ | 6̣ 1 2 3 |
5 3 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 | 2 3 5 6 | 5 - |
2 3 5 3 | 2 1 6̣ 1 | 2 3 2 1 | 6̣ 5̣ 6̣ 1 |
2 - | 0 0 ||
```
Northern fire and energy. Double tonguing and flutter tonguing. The fast section captures the vitality of Shaanxi folk life — weddings, festivals, the energy of the people.

### Song 4: 愁空山 (Sorrowful Empty Mountain)
By Guo Wenjing (郭文景, 1992). A contemporary masterwork for dizi and orchestra. Grade 9+. Pushes the instrument to its limits with extended techniques.

**Opening**
```
愁空山 — Opening (approximate, simplified)
1=D  散板

pp
5̣ - - - - | 0 0 0 0 | 6̣ - - - - | 0 0 0 0 |
1 - - - - - | 2 - 1 - 6̣ - | 5̣ - - - - - |
0 0 0 0 | 0 0 0 0 |

(gradually emerging)
6̣ - 1 - | 2 - - 3 | 2 - 1 - | 6̣ - - - |
5̣ 6̣ 1 2 | 3 - 2 1 | 6̣ - 5̣ - | 6̣ - - - - |

(This piece requires the full published score for accurate study.
The notation here is a simplified excerpt for familiarization.)
```
This is an aspirational piece. Study the recording by Tang Junqiao (唐俊乔). It represents the frontier of dizi artistry. The opening emerges from near-silence — single notes floating in empty space, like echoes in an empty mountain valley. The piece uses multiphonics, extreme dynamics, flutter tonguing, and techniques that push the dizi beyond its traditional boundaries.

After learning the full piece, revisit the final section where the slow theme returns transformed:


The theme returns, but now suffused with the warmth of spring. What was fragile and searching in the introduction is now fulfilled and radiant. The final notes fade to silence — the orchid blooms, and the music dissolves into stillness.

## Finding a Teacher

At this level, self-study reaches its limits. Consider:

### In-Person
- Chinese music schools or cultural centers in your city
- University Chinese music programs
- Chinese community organizations

### Online
- Search for dizi teachers offering video lessons
- TENG Academy (Singapore) offers online programs
- Some Chinese conservatory professors offer remote lessons

### Masterclasses and Workshops
- Chinese music festivals and conferences
- Summer intensive programs
- Visiting artist workshops at universities

### What to Look for in a Teacher
- Performance background (not just theoretical knowledge)
- Understanding of both northern and southern styles
- Patience with adult learners
- Can demonstrate techniques (not just describe them)
- Ideally, trained at a Chinese conservatory

## Exam Preparation (CCOM Grade 7-9)

If pursuing formal grading:

### CCOM Grade 7
- Scales in all modes
- Etudes with mixed techniques
- 2 contrasting pieces (e.g., one northern, one southern)
- Sight-reading

### CCOM Grade 8-9
- All Grade 7 requirements at higher standard
- More demanding repertoire
- Musical interpretation evaluated (not just technical accuracy)
- Original composition or arrangement (Grade 9)

## Joining Ensembles

### Chinese Orchestra (民族管弦乐团/国乐团)
- Dizi is a core instrument in Chinese orchestra
- Usually divided into bangdi and qudi sections
- Requires reading conductor cues, following dynamics, blending tone
- Search for Chinese orchestras in your city — many welcome adult amateurs

### Silk and Bamboo (丝竹) Ensemble
- Small chamber group: dizi, erhu, pipa, zhongruan, yangqin
- Jiangnan sizhu (江南丝竹) is the classic tradition
- More intimate, more ornamental freedom
- Each player ornaments the melody differently, creating a rich heterophonic texture

### Cross-Genre Collaboration
- Jazz fusion with Chinese instruments
- World music ensembles
- Film and media scoring
- Electronic music collaboration — dizi samples and live processing

## The Lifelong Journey

At this level, you've gone from knowing nothing about music to playing concert-level dizi. But mastery is a lifelong pursuit:

- **Keep practicing long tones** — yes, still. Even Lu Chunling practiced long tones daily into his 90s
- **Learn new pieces** — the repertoire is vast and constantly growing
- **Listen constantly** — to masters, to other instruments, to nature
- **Teach others** — teaching deepens your own understanding
- **Explore composition** — create your own pieces
- **Study related instruments** — xiao (洞箫), bawu (巴乌), hulusi (葫芦丝)
- **Connect with Chinese culture** — music, poetry, painting, philosophy are deeply interconnected
- **Record yourself** — listen back critically, track your progress over months and years
